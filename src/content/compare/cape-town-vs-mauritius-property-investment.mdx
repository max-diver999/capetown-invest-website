## Cape Town vs Mauritius: The Core Trade-Off

For a foreign buyer weighing two Indian Ocean lifestyle markets, Cape Town and Mauritius represent different strategies rather than two versions of the same beach bet. Mauritius is the scheme-gated, residency-linked island play: approved PDS and legacy RES or IRS stock, property-tied residence permits above Economic Development Board thresholds, tourism-driven rentals, and prices often quoted in US dollars within regulated developments. Cape Town is the open-market, semigration-powered mainland play: no foreign surcharge, freedom to buy prime Atlantic Seaboard or City Bowl stock without a scheme wrapper, modeled coastal yields of 6 to 9%, but no visa attached to the purchase and income earned in a volatile rand. Get this framing right before you compare listings, because the decision turns less on which coastline is prettier and more on whether you prioritise residency, scheme structure, or open-market yield and provincial growth.

The fault line is restriction versus flexibility. Mauritius channels non-citizen capital into IRS, RES, PDS, and Smart City envelopes with minimum investments that double as residency qualifiers, delivering island tourism liquidity and a managed currency, but capping where you can shop and often compressing yields into a 4 to 6% gross band once levies and seasonality are honest. Cape Town lets foreigners buy broadly on the same transfer duty scale as locals, models stronger income in nodes such as Sea Point, and sits inside a Western Cape market up about 179.6% from 2010 to September 2025, powered by semigration rather than resort arrivals alone, but offers no property-linked visa and exposes you to rand moves. One market trades scheme gates for residency; the other trades currency volatility for open-market access and deeper domestic demand.

## Tax and Acquisition: Open Cape Town vs Scheme-Gated Mauritius

The clearest structural divergence is not a headline nationality tax but where foreigners are allowed to buy. South Africa imposes no foreign buyer surcharge, no additional acquisition tax, and no stamp-duty premium anywhere, so a foreigner purchasing in Cape Town pays the same transfer duty scale as a local on freehold or sectional title stock across the Atlantic Seaboard, City Bowl, or Winelands. Mauritius also avoids a crude foreigner penalty, yet non-citizens may only acquire property within approved Integrated Resort Scheme legacy projects, Real Estate Scheme legacy stock, active Property Development Scheme launches, or qualifying Smart City schemes registered with the Economic Development Board. That scheme boundary effectively sets a minimum ticket and a due diligence path unlike Cape Town's open market.

| Acquisition factor | Cape Town | Mauritius |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Foreign buyer surcharge | None | None on nationality |
| Where foreigners may buy | Open market citywide | IRS, RES, PDS, Smart City only |
| Typical minimum entry | Median near R1.9m, area-dependent | Scheme thresholds, often USD 375,000+ for PDS residency track |
| Main transfer taxes | Transfer duty on national scale | Registration duty plus scheme levies |
| Annual wealth tax on home | None on residential ownership | None, but scheme service charges apply |
| Residence permit link | None | Qualifying scheme purchase can support permit |

Cape Town minimises friction at the point of entry and maximises choice of suburb and building. Mauritius concentrates foreign demand into regulated envelopes where developers pre-clear eligibility, which simplifies compliance but removes the option to pick up a random Sea Point equivalent outside the scheme list. For a buyer who already intended a USD 400,000 island lifestyle base with residency, Mauritius scheme stock is feature, not bug. For a buyer who wants to hunt yield in an established urban node at R3m to R8m without a developer gate, Cape Town is the cleaner fit. Verify current PDS minimums and registration duty with Mauritian professionals before you treat any threshold as fixed.

## Yield Comparison: Modeled Cape Town Income vs Island Tourism Gross

Yield is where Cape Town usually leads on modeled numbers, but the currencies and tax treatments differ sharply. Cape Town coastal income nodes model around 6 to 9% gross, with a Sea Point one-bedroom modeling roughly 9.7% gross and 7.5% net before you stress-test vacancy and maintenance, while rental income remains taxable in South Africa and earned in rand. Mauritius scheme and resort rentals often model nearer 4 to 6% gross once marketing gloss is stripped, with winter and summer tourism seasonality, scheme management fees, and furniture packs compressing net outcomes. Serious investors rebuild net yield with levies, letting commission, occupancy assumptions, and tax, not developer brochure gross.

| Yield factor | Cape Town | Mauritius |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Modeled gross range | ~6 to 9% in income nodes | ~4 to 6% in many scheme rentals |
| Best modeled Cape Town case | Sea Point ~9.7% gross, ~7.5% net | Scheme and villa dependent |
| Income tax | Taxable in South Africa | Mauritian rules apply to local income |
| Income currency | Rand | Often USD or EUR quoted, settled with MUR costs |
| Demand driver | Semigration plus urban tenants | Island tourism plus expat lettings |
| Seasonality | Moderate, year-round city | Stronger peak-trough tourism swing |

The honest comparison is taxable rand income with semigration-backed tenant depth versus scheme-gated island lettings with residency upside but thinner off-season occupancy. A Cape Town 7.5% net Sea Point model is strong but exposed to South African rental tax and rand conversion. A Mauritius 5% gross scheme return may look stable in dollars but must survive shoulder-season voids and service charges. ## Currency: Volatile Rand vs Managed Mauritian Rupee

Currency shapes both entry price and repatriation story. The South African rand is volatile relative to the US dollar, euro, and pound, which makes Cape Town entry cheaper when the rand is weak and amplifies hard-currency gains if the rand recovers from stressed levels, but erodes the value of rand rent and capital when it slides further. Foreign buyers must record incoming funds through an authorised dealer bank with non-resident endorsement for clean repatriation, a process covered in [Buying Cape Town Property as a Foreigner](/guides/buy-cape-town-property-foreigner/).

The Mauritian rupee is managed and generally less volatile than the rand, offering relative calm for local operating costs. Scheme marketing, however, often prices units in US dollars or euros, which partially hard-currencies the investment even while utilities and staff run in MUR. That hybrid can suit buyers who want residency and a dollar-denominated ticket without full emerging-market currency swing, but it removes the deep discount a weak rand gives in Cape Town today. Cape Town is the higher-risk, higher-optionality currency play; Mauritius is moderate stability with scheme-level hard currency quoting.

| Currency factor | Cape Town (ZAR) | Mauritius (MUR) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Volatility vs USD | High | Lower, managed |
| Effect on entry | Weak rand cuts hard-currency price | Scheme prices often USD-linked |
| Rental income FX | Rand converts with full swing | Mixed USD rents, MUR costs |
| Repatriation | Exchange control with recording | Scheme and bank rules apply |
| Upside case | Rand recovery amplifies returns | Stability, less rebound option |

## Residency: Mauritius Property Link vs Cape Town Clean Investment

Residency is the decisive non-financial fork. Mauritius allows a residence permit pathway when a non-citizen purchases qualifying property at or above Economic Development Board thresholds in an approved PDS development or eligible legacy RES or IRS project, subject to compliance, renewals, and professional advice. Smart City schemes can offer parallel routes with their own minimums. The property is not automatic citizenship, but it is a genuine immigration anchor that Dubai and Cape Town treat differently.

South Africa has never tied residency to property. Buying in Cape Town confers no visa, work right, or permanent residence shortcut. Non-residents enter through standard visa categories unrelated to the deed. If your goal includes a renewable island base with tax and lifestyle planning, Mauritius scheme property belongs in the conversation with an immigration lawyer. If residency is irrelevant and you want pure investment plus semigration exposure, Cape Town's clean separation keeps the asset decision honest.

| Residency factor | Cape Town | Mauritius |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Property grants residency | No | Yes, in qualifying schemes |
| Main immigration route | Standard visa categories | PDS, RES, IRS, Smart City thresholds |
| Link to purchase | None | Direct when conditions met |
| Best for residency goal | No | Yes, with scheme budget |

## Liquidity: Semigration Depth vs Island Tourism Resale

Liquidity is often misunderstood as simply tourism footfall. Cape Town's premium resale depth in 2025 reflected R11.3bn in Atlantic Seaboard and City Bowl sales with foreigners at roughly 25% of value, underpinned by semigration from Gauteng, corporate relocations, and local upgrade chains that continue when international arrivals soften. That domestic depth gives Sea Point income stock a broader exit pool than a single-resort narrative implies.

Mauritius liquidity is real but narrower and scheme-specific. Approved developments trade among international buyers seeking lifestyle, tax planning, and residency, with active resale in established RES and PDS names. Turnover can slow when the Economic Development Board adjusts minimums, when new supply launches nearby, or when global buyers pause island second-home purchases. Tourism supports short-term letting, not automatically long-term exit timing. Cape Town favours semigration breadth; Mauritius favours niche international scheme demand. | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary demand engine | Semigration plus local upgrades | Tourism plus expat scheme buyers |
| Foreign buyer share | ~25% of premium Seaboard value | Concentrated in approved schemes |
| Exit breadth | Citywide nodes plus Winelands | Scheme and developer specific |
| Cycle risk | National politics and rand | Global leisure and scheme rule changes |

## Pros and Cons: Side by Side

Both markets can work for disciplined foreign buyers; the question is which drawbacks you prefer to manage.

| Market | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cape Town | No foreign surcharge; open-market choice; modeled 6 to 9% yields; +179.6% provincial growth; semigration liquidity | Rand volatility; rental income taxable; no residency link |
| Mauritius | Property-linked residence permit in schemes; island tourism; managed MUR; hard-currency scheme pricing | Scheme-only purchase path; yields often 4 to 6% gross; seasonality; minimum investment gates |

Cape Town suits buyers who want urban coastal income with provincial growth tailwinds and freedom to choose any suburb. Mauritius suits buyers who budget for scheme minimums and value a residence permit anchor alongside island lifestyle, accepting lower modeled yield and corridor-specific resale.

## Verdict: Scheme Residency vs Open-Market Yield

Cape Town and Mauritius answer different questions. Mauritius answers where do I combine island lifestyle, scheme governance, and a property-linked residence permit, accepting IRS, RES, PDS, or Smart City gates, tourism seasonality, and yields often modeled nearer 4 to 6% gross. Cape Town answers where do I buy on the open market with no foreign surcharge, model stronger coastal income around 6 to 9%, and ride semigration inside a Western Cape market up about 179.6% from 2010 to September 2025, accepting rand volatility and no visa from the deed.

Neither market is universally superior. Residency-motivated buyers with scheme budgets should weigh Mauritius with immigration and tax counsel. Yield and flexibility buyers should weigh Cape Town with refreshed MODELED math in the [Cape Town Rental Yield Guide](/guides/cape-town-rental-yield-guide/). Decide whether you are buying a regulated island base or an open-market semigration asset first, then shortlist accordingly.
