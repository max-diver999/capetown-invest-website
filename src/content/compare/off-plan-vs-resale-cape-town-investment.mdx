## Off-plan vs resale Cape Town: which path fits your goal?

Off-plan versus resale is not a question of which is cheaper. It is a question of whether you prioritise immediate rental income and known costs or a locked launch price, VAT treatment and a new build with warranty cover while accepting delivery risk.

In Cape Town's 2026 market, completed sectional-title stock stays tight in nodes like Sea Point and Century City, while municipal plan approvals have softened nationally, which keeps some buyers on developer launch lists. Resale suits buy-to-let investors who model net yield from month one. Off-plan suits buyers who can fund deposits into trust accounts, wait through construction, and vet developers carefully.

## How does VAT versus transfer duty change your all-in cost in Cape Town?

Off-plan from a VAT-registered developer carries 15 percent VAT included in the price with no transfer duty line item, while resale from an existing owner pays progressive transfer duty on the purchase price with no VAT on standard residential sales between private parties. On a R3,000,000 ticket, resale transfer duty adds roughly R146,000 that the off-plan VAT path avoids as a separate charge, but that does not mean off-plan is R146,000 cheaper overall because developers price VAT into the launch number and off-plan carries deposits, waiting, and levy uncertainty. Foreign buyers face no extra surcharge on either path and the same VAT or duty rules apply as for locals. Always model all-in including conveyancing, bond costs, and levy projections using the cost of buying guide before you choose based on headline price alone.

The clearest financial difference between off-plan and resale in South Africa is tax on acquisition. They do not stack; you pay one regime or the other.

Off-plan (new build from VAT-registered developer): the price is VAT-inclusive at 15%. No transfer duty is charged on that sale. The developer accounts for VAT to SARS.

Resale (existing owner): the buyer pays progressive transfer duty on the purchase price. No VAT on standard residential resale between private parties.

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Off-plan from developer | 15% VAT included | Built into price | No transfer duty line item |
| Resale sectional title | Transfer duty scale | Buyer on registration | Duty rises with price bands |
| Developer resale of unsold unit | Usually VAT if developer sells | Confirm status | Ask if VAT or duty applies |

## Why does NHBRC warranty matter more on off-plan than resale?

The National Home Builders Registration Council enrolment requirement protects off-plan buyers with 3 months of roof leak cover, 12 months for workmanship and material defects, and 5 years for major structural defects all measured from occupation, while resale stock is usually sold voetstoots with no NHBRC cover unless the home is still inside the warranty window and properly enrolled at construction. Off-plan buyers pay deposits 12 to 36 months before they can inspect walls or waterproofing, so NHBRC enrolment in writing before the first deposit protects against developer insolvency and builder failure, whereas resale buyers can inspect immediately and rely on a building survey to catch defects before transfer. Resale homes built more than 5 years ago carry no NHBRC protection, so inspect thoroughly, budget for repairs, or buy off-plan for fresh warranty cover that backstops the most expensive failures for the full 60 months after handover.

The National Home Builders Registration Council (NHBRC) enrolment requirement is a structural protection that mainly benefits off-plan and new-build buyers.

On off-plan, the developer must enrol the home with NHBRC and use a registered builder. You receive warranty cover against major structural defects for five years from occupation, plus shorter cover for roof leaks and certain other defects. Before you sign an OTP, confirm enrolment in writing and read the [NHBRC warranty South Africa guide](/guides/nhbrc-warranty-south-africa-new-build/).

Resale stock is typically sold voetstoots (as-is). Unless the unit is still inside an active NHBRC window with valid enrolment, you rely on inspection, not warranty. That shifts risk to your due diligence budget and snag list before transfer.

| Factor | Off-plan / new build | Resale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Structural warranty | NHBRC 5-year cover when enrolled | Usually none |
| Defect remedy | Developer / NHBRC process | Negotiate before transfer or accept |
| Inspection timing | Snagging at completion | Pre-offer inspection critical |
| Hidden maintenance | Lower early years | Depends on prior owner |

For investors who cannot visit Cape Town often, NHBRC cover is a meaningful offset to off-plan waiting time. For hands-on buyers who inspect thoroughly, quality resale in a well-run body corporate can still be rational.

## How do body corporate levies differ between off-plan launches and resale schemes?

Sectional-title levies can make or break net yield, and off-plan versus resale differ sharply in how much you know before you commit. On resale you request audited body corporate financials, the levy schedule, reserve fund balance, and any planned special levies, so you see what owners actually pay today. On off-plan the developer sets a founding budget before the scheme is owner-controlled, marketing levies are often optimistic, and levies frequently rise once owners fund reserves, security upgrades, lift maintenance, and insurance properly after handover.

Rule of thumb for off-plan underwriting: add 15% to 30% to the marketed levy in your net yield model unless you have evidence from the developer's completed schemes that launch budgets held. On resale, use the actual levy on the statement. Levy load interacts with suburb choice: luxury Atlantic Seaboard blocks carry heavier common-area costs than Century City or Blouberg modern schemes, regardless of off-plan or resale. ## When does resale deliver rental income faster than off-plan?

For buy-to-let investors, time to first rent is often the decisive variable between paths. Resale follows accepted offer, bond approval if needed, conveyancing, and Deeds Office registration, then letting in a typical 8 to 12 week span if uncontested, so MODELED gross yield starts shortly after registration. Off-plan runs OTP at launch, phased deposits into trust, construction for 12 to 36 months, snagging, transfer, then let, which means zero rental income during the build unless you finance the wait from offshore cash.

Consider a MODELED 8% gross yield on R3 million (R240,000 per year). Waiting 24 months on off-plan defers roughly R480,000 of gross rent before costs, while resale might collect much of that sooner. Off-plan must compensate with price advantage, tax efficiency, lower early maintenance, and capital growth during the build, none of which is guaranteed. Short-stay investors on the Atlantic Seaboard should also confirm body corporate short-term rental rules before choosing either path, because a resale unit with STR permission in hand can beat off-plan with uncertain rules at launch.

## Price, deposit and bond mechanics compared

Off-plan pricing is fixed at launch relative to expected completed value. You often pay deposits in stages (for example 10% on signing, further tranches at slab and roof). Bonds for non-residents are typically capped near 50% loan-to-value and register near completion, not at OTP. Cash planning must cover deposits plus the gap until the bond draws down.

Resale pricing is negotiated against current comparables. You pay transfer duty, negotiate voetstoots, and can bond against a registered asset immediately. A cash buyer can close quickly; a bonded buyer runs bond approval in parallel with conveyancing.

| Mechanic | Off-plan | Resale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Price discovery | Launch list | Comparable sales |
| Deposit | Phased, trust | Often 10% acceptance |
| Bond timing | Near completion | With transfer |
| Negotiation | Limited on launch | Offer vs asking |
| Show unit risk | Spec may differ | What you see |

## Developer and voetstoots risk: who you trust

Off-plan concentrates risk in the developer. Resale concentrates risk in the prior owner and building maintenance.

Off-plan red flags: no NHBRC registration, deposits outside conveyancer trust, vague completion dates, no penalty for delay, weak track record on prior schemes, or insolvency rumours.

Resale red flags: damp, DIY electrical work, special levies pending, disputed body corporate governance, or STR bans when you plan Airbnb.

Choose off-plan if you can wait, you trust the developer after deep checks, launch pricing beats completed comparables on an all-in basis, and NHBRC plus VAT structure matter to your tax planning.

Many portfolios hold both: resale income unit now, off-plan in a precinct you like at launch pricing.

## Foreign buyers: same rules, different cash flow

Foreigners may buy off-plan or resale on identical legal footing: FICA, authorised dealer inflows, non-resident endorsement for repatriation, and roughly 50% LTV on local bonds.

Off-plan is harder operationally for overseas buyers because you sign remotely, fund staged deposits, and only register a bond near completion. Powers of attorney and apostilled documents are standard; see [buy Cape Town property remotely](/guides/buy-cape-town-property-remotely/).

Resale is simpler for foreign yield investors: inspect virtually or via agent, offer, bond, transfer, appoint managing agent, collect rent.

Neither path charges a foreign-buyer premium. Compare all-in costs only.

## Insider tip: levy projections on off-plan often underestimate by 20 to 40 percent

Developers quote projected monthly levies at off-plan launch based on a founding budget that assumes perfect occupancy, no deferred maintenance, and minimal reserve fund contributions. Those projections often rise 20 to 40 percent within 12 to 24 months after owners take control of the body corporate and fund the 10-year maintenance plan properly. A Century City off-plan scheme marketed at R2,800 per month levy in 2023 reached R3,900 per month by 2025 after the first AGM voted to fund backup power, repaint common areas, and top up reserves that the developer seeded at the statutory minimum. That R1,100 per month increase compounds to R13,200 per year, which on a R2.5 million unit drops net yield by roughly 0.5 percentage points before you factor in rates and management costs.

Resale schemes have audited financials you can verify today, so you know exactly what R4,500 per month means and can track levy inflation over the past three years to stress-test future increases. Off-plan buyers depend on the developer's track record across completed schemes to sanity-check the founding budget, and serious buyers request levy actuals from three similar schemes the developer delivered in the past five years before accepting the launch projection. A developer who consistently delivers levies within 10 percent of forecast earns trust; a developer whose completed schemes run 30 to 50 percent above launch projections should trigger deeper questions about costing discipline and whether the founding budget is realistic or marketing.

Off-plan buyers also face uncertainty on special levies for capital upgrades the founding budget did not anticipate. A Sea Point scheme that handed over in 2024 raised a R28,000 per unit special levy in 2026 to install a communal generator after Stage 6 load-shedding proved the original electrical design inadequate, and owners had no choice but to pay or sell into a softening market. Resale buyers see special levy history in the body corporate minutes and can confirm whether a major upgrade is imminent before transfer, while off-plan buyers discover special levies only after they own the unit and the body corporate votes to raise them. Factor levy uncertainty into your off-plan cash flow model by stress-testing a 25 to 35 percent increase on the developer's projection and confirming whether that still clears your net yield hurdle before you pay the first deposit.

Off-plan: delay, developer failure, spec downgrade, levy shock, bond timing gap, market softening before completion.

Resale: voetstoots defects, deferred maintenance, special levies, outdated electrical and plumbing, STR restrictions.
